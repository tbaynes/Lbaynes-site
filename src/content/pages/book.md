  - text: "In <em>Between Interpretation and Imagination</em>, Leslie Baynes brings a New Testament scholar&rsquo;s tools to the question. She reconstructs Lewis&rsquo;s scriptural formation from his Belfast childhood through his Oxford years to the final Cambridge decade, identifies the figures who shaped his interpretive instincts &mdash; Charles Gore, James Moffatt, and others largely forgotten outside academic theology &mdash; and examines the marginalia Lewis left in his own copies of commentaries and Bibles. Many of those notes appear here in print for the first time."
    accent: false
  - text: "The book is direct where Lewis was wrong and generous where he was brilliant. Baynes argues that Lewis&rsquo;s nonfiction engagement with academic biblical scholarship &mdash; including his famous essay <em>&ldquo;Modern Theology and Biblical Criticism&rdquo;</em> and the <em>&ldquo;Liar, Lunatic, Lord&rdquo;</em> argument &mdash; is weaker than his admirers believe. But she finds that Lewis&rsquo;s greatest gift as a reader of Scripture was never exegetical. It was imaginative."
    accent: false
  - text: "The Chronicles of Narnia, she shows, are saturated with biblical and classical allusion in ways even devoted readers have missed: the talking animals rooted in rabbinic tradition, the Calormene theology drawn from ancient sources, Aslan&rsquo;s death and return carrying more than the obvious cross-shaped echo. It is, in the end, a book about how a literary genius reads a sacred book &mdash; and about what the rest of us can learn from both his failures and his triumphs."
